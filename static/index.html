◈ SPX Hedge Ranker
$5M equity hedge  ·  Roth IRA puts  ·  ranked by EPR

What this tool does

Fetches the live CBOE SPX options chain (delayed ~15 min), scores every put by Expected Payoff Ratio (EPR), and ranks them so you can find the most efficient tail-hedge contracts for a $5M equity portfolio held in a Roth IRA.

Workflow

  1. Configure your probability model on the Model Builder tab (GARCH/EP model runs automatically in the background). Switching back here applies your model automatically.
  2. Adjust Roth multiplier and Contracts to match your situation, then sort by EPR.
  3. Check Sprd% and vs Theo% to filter out illiquid or unreliably-priced contracts.
  4. Use Ann% to sanity-check total cost. A 1%/yr insurance budget on a $5M portfolio is $50K/yr.

Grid columns

Option identification

Expiry
Expiration date (YYYY-MM-DD).
Ses
AM = standard SPX (cash-settled at open on expiry day). PM = SPXW weekly (settled at close). AM has less pin-risk for multi-month holds.
DTE
Calendar days to expiration (from CBOE expiry date). The filter defaults to 180–730 calendar days (6–24 months). Not the same as the Model Builder's trading-day DTE (252 trading days = 1 year ≈ 365 calendar days).
Strike
Option strike price in SPX index points.
OTM%
(strike / SPX − 1) × 100. A value of −20% means the strike is 20% below current SPX — roughly where a "deep correction" scenario pays off.

Market data

Mid
Mid-market price = (bid + ask) / 2, in SPX points. Multiply by 100 for dollar cost per contract.
Sprd%
Bid-ask spread as % of mid. Shown amber above 20% — the mid is unreliable at that width; expect to pay more than mid to fill.
IV
Implied volatility from the CBOE model. Higher IV = more expensive. Compare across strikes and expiries to find relatively cheap contracts.
Delta
$ change in option price per $1 move in SPX. Negative for puts. −0.15 ≈ 15% probability of finishing in-the-money (rough approximation).
Theta/d
Daily time decay in dollars per contract. LEAPS have slow theta — a key advantage for set-and-forget hedges.
OI
Open interest (total open contracts). Higher = better liquidity. Filter minimum is 100.
Vol
Today's trading volume. Thin volume is normal for LEAPS but confirms whether the mid reflects real activity.

Sizing

Cost 1c
Total premium for one contract = mid × 100.
Cost Nc
Total cost for your full position of N contracts.

Scenario payoffs

Gross payoff per contract if SPX falls by the stated amount from today's level. Deterministic — no probability weighting.

SPX −25%
"Mild crash" — your $5M portfolio would drop ~22% beta-adjusted.
SPX −40%
"Deep crash" — roughly 2008-level drawdown.
SPX −55%
"Severe crash" — Great Depression / dot-com scale. Deeply OTM puts may pay nothing at −25% but a large multiple of cost at −55%.

Expected value metrics

E[Pay]
Probability-weighted expected payoff across all scenarios, Roth-adjusted: Σ(Pi × payoffi) × Roth multiplier. This is what the put is "worth" to you given your probability model.
E[Net]
E[Pay] minus Cost 1c. Positive = positive expected value at your assumed probabilities. Most market puts have negative E[Net] at market-implied crash probabilities; a positive value means you believe crashes are underpriced.
EPR (primary sort key)
Expected Payoff Ratio = E[Pay] / Cost. The main ranking signal.
  • > 2.0 (green): excellent value — more than $2 of expected payoff per $1 spent
  • 1.0 – 2.0 (yellow): break-even to modestly positive
  • < 1.0: expensive relative to your model, but may still be worth buying for pure tail protection
EPR is most useful for comparing puts against each other — the absolute level depends on your probability inputs.
CrEff
Crash Efficiency — same as EPR but counting only crash scenarios in the numerator. Answers: "how efficiently does this put buy crash protection specifically?" A deeply OTM put has high CrEff but lower EPR. A put closer to the money has higher EPR but lower CrEff. Use CrEff when you care primarily about tail protection rather than overall expected value.

Cost metrics

Ann%
Annualized cost of the full position as % of the $5M portfolio = (Cost Nc / $5M) × (365 / DTE) × 100. Good tail insurance typically runs 0.5–1.5%/yr. Above 2% is expensive for a set-and-forget hedge.
vs Theo%
How much you're paying vs CBOE's own theoretical model price: (mid − theo) / theo × 100.
  • >+10%: overpaying relative to CBOE's model — wide spread or stale quote
  • Near 0: normal for liquid strikes
  • <−10%: cheaper than CBOE's model, rare but worth noting

Filters

DTE min / max
Filter by calendar days to expiration (e.g. 180 ≈ 6 months, 365 ≈ 1 year, 730 ≈ 2 years). Default 180–730. Widen to see shorter-dated puts, which are cheaper but require more frequent rolling. Note: the Model Builder tab uses trading days (252/yr) — a different scale.
OI min
Minimum open interest. Default 100. Raise to 500+ if you want only the most liquid strikes.
Vol min
Minimum today's volume. Useful for confirming that a mid-price reflects real trading, but LEAPS often trade lightly — set to 0 unless you need same-day fills.
OTM% min / max
Filter by how far out of the money the strike is. Default max is 0% (puts only). Set min to e.g. −30% to exclude very deep strikes; set max to e.g. −10% to exclude near-the-money puts.
Session
AM = standard SPX (cash-settled at open). PM = SPXW weekly (settled at close). AM is generally preferred for multi-month holds to avoid close-of-day gamma risk near expiry.

Scenario model

The Scenario model selector controls how crash probabilities are assigned to each option. GARCH/EP (default) uses the full simulation from the Model Builder tab and is the most rigorous option — P(crash) and Horizon inputs are greyed out when it is active.

Simpler fallback models (no GARCH required):

survival
You specify P(crash) and Horizon. Crash probability scales to each option's DTE using a constant-hazard formula: a 6-month put gets a lower P(crash) than an 18-month put, proportional to the time window. Best simple approach for cross-expiry comparison.
flat
The same P(crash) applied to every option regardless of DTE. Useful as a quick reference or when you want to state a probability for a specific expiry window directly.
P(crash)
(survival / flat only) Probability of ≥20% SPX drawdown over the horizon. Default 0.55.
Horizon (months)
(survival only) Reference window for P(crash). Does not affect which options appear — use DTE filter for that.

Other parameters

Roth multiplier
Tax advantage of a Roth IRA. At a 20% capital-gains rate, $1 of Roth payoff is worth $1.25 in taxable-equivalent terms. Raise if you're in a higher bracket.
Contracts (0 = auto)
Number of contracts. 0 auto-sizes from portfolio beta: N = ($5M × β) / (SPX × 100). At SPX ≈ 6,500 this gives ~7 contracts for a full hedge.
Symbol / Index beta
Load a different options chain (NDX, RUT). Index beta scales the SPX crash return to the target index — e.g. NDX historically falls harder than SPX in tech-driven crashes, use ~1.3.

What the Model Builder does

The Model Builder lets you construct a probability distribution over future SPX paths that blends two inputs: a statistical prior from a GARCH simulation and your own subjective crash views. The resulting distribution is used to score every put option on the Options Grid tab.

Workflow

  1. Wait for the GARCH simulation to finish (~15s). The status bar shows "Ready" when done.
  2. Review the YOUR VIEW VS MODEL / MARKET table — it shows GARCH prior and market-implied probabilities as a baseline.
  3. Enter your crash probability views in the bucket table, adjust the EP Confidence slider, and watch the charts update.
  4. Switch to the Options Grid tab — your model is committed automatically and the grid refreshes.

The GARCH prior

GJR-GARCH (Glosten-Jagannathan-Runkle GARCH) is a statistical volatility model fitted to 30+ years of SPX daily returns. It captures two well-documented features of equity markets:

  • Volatility clustering — calm periods tend to stay calm; turbulent periods tend to stay turbulent. GARCH models this by letting today's volatility depend on recent squared returns.
  • Leverage effect — down moves increase future volatility more than up moves of the same size. The GJR asymmetric term captures this asymmetry explicitly.

The server simulates thousands of daily SPX paths over a 2-year horizon using this fitted model. Each path represents one possible future. The collection of paths forms the GARCH prior — a probability distribution grounded in historical market behavior.

Annual drift sets the long-run expected return used in the simulation. Default 7%/yr (conservative long-run real return). The GARCH-fitted drift (~13–14%) reflects survivorship-biased historical data and overstates likely future returns. Set to 0 for a flat, risk-neutral-style projection.

Re-init simulation reruns the GARCH fit and path generation from scratch using the current SPX price and your chosen drift. Use this if you change the drift or if the price has moved significantly since the last init.

Entropy Pooling — blending your views

Entropy Pooling (EP) is a technique for combining a prior probability distribution with subjective views. It finds the probability distribution that is as close as possible to the prior (in the sense of minimum relative entropy, or KL-divergence) while exactly satisfying your specified constraints.

In practice, EP reweights the GARCH paths: paths consistent with your views get more weight; paths inconsistent with them get less. The shape of the distribution remains GARCH-like — you're tilting it, not replacing it.

The bucket table — entering your views

Each row in the table states a constraint on the 2-year running minimum of SPX:

P(SPX drops below [Level] at any point in the next 2 years) = [Cum Prob]

For example, "SPX 4170 → 0.25" means you believe there is a 25% chance SPX will touch 4,170 or lower within 2 years. Enter rows in ascending order of level; the "Move %" column shows how far each level is from the current spot.

You do not need to fill in every row — EP interpolates between your constraints and uses the GARCH prior outside the range you specify. Leave rows at their defaults if you have no strong view.

EP Confidence slider

Controls how strongly your views are imposed:

  • 0.0 — pure GARCH prior; your bucket entries are ignored entirely
  • 1.0 — views fully imposed; paths are reweighted to exactly match your stated probabilities
  • 0.75 (default) — a blend: your views shift the distribution but the GARCH prior acts as a regularizer, preventing extreme reweighting

Reduce confidence if the EP solution looks too extreme (e.g. very heavy tails or very thin tails relative to what you intended).

The market-implied distribution (B-L / RN)

The Market (B-L) view is derived from live SPX options prices using the Breeden-Litzenberger formula, which extracts a risk-neutral probability density from the cross-section of option prices at each expiry. Specifically, the second derivative of the call price with respect to strike gives the market's implied probability density over terminal SPX levels.

This density is used in two ways:

  • Distribution surface chart — the blue violin profiles show the market-implied density at each options expiry date. The width of the violin at each SPX level is proportional to the market's probability mass at that level.
  • Market (RN) column — the GARCH paths are reweighted so that the terminal distribution matches the market-implied density, and then the 2-year running-minimum probabilities are recomputed. This gives a market-consistent view directly comparable to your GARCH Prior and Your View columns.

Important: the market-implied distribution is risk-neutral, not physical. Because investors pay a fear premium for downside protection, the risk-neutral density assigns higher probability to large drawdowns than would a physical (real-world) estimate. The Market (RN) column will therefore generally show higher crash probabilities than the GARCH Prior — this is expected and not a model error.

The charts

YOUR VIEW VS MODEL / MARKET table

Compares three probability estimates side by side for a set of SPX drawdown levels:

Your View
P(2yr running min ≤ level) under the EP-reweighted distribution. Reflects your bucket inputs blended with the GARCH prior at your chosen confidence.
GARCH Prior
The raw GARCH simulation probability — no view blending applied. Your baseline before any subjective adjustment.
Market (RN)
The market-implied probability from B-L reweighting of GARCH paths. Incorporates the risk premium embedded in SPX options prices. Generally higher than GARCH Prior due to the fear premium.

Terminal Distribution at Selected DTE

Shows the probability density of SPX at a single future date (chosen from the selector). The selector uses trading days (252/yr): 63 ≈ 3 months, 126 ≈ 6 months, 252 = 1 year, 378 ≈ 18 months, 504 = 2 years. The blue curve is the GARCH prior; the white curve is Your View after EP reweighting. Distributions that agree closely mean your views are similar to the GARCH baseline at that horizon.

Representative Paths by Quintile

Five SPX paths, one from each quintile of 2-year drawdown severity. Quintile 1 (red) is the worst crash path; Quintile 5 (green) is the best bull path. Line weight reflects EP probability mass — thicker lines carry more weight under your view. After EP reweighting, the crash quintiles become thicker if you entered bearish views.

Distribution Surface

Shows the full SPX probability distribution across all future time horizons, with three view options:

Market (B-L)
Blue violin profiles: the market-implied density at each options expiry date, derived from live CBOE prices. The Mkt P10/P50/P90 lines connect the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles of those densities across expiries. This is always the base layer.
My View
Overlays three lines (EP P10/P50/P90) from the EP-reweighted GARCH paths on top of the market violins. Lets you see how your crash view compares to what the market is pricing in.
GARCH Prior
Overlays GARCH P10/P50/P90 (without EP adjustment) on top of the market violins. Shows the raw model baseline.

The x-axis is months from today (0–24). The dotted red line is the current SPX spot price.

SPX Hedge Ranker — Reference Guide

What this tool does

Fetches the live CBOE SPX options chain (delayed ~15 min), scores every put by Expected Payoff Ratio (EPR), and ranks them so you can find the most efficient tail-hedge contracts for a $5M equity portfolio held in a Roth IRA.

Recommended workflow

  1. Open the Model Builder tab. The GJR-GARCH simulation starts automatically in the background (~15s). While it loads, you can read the prior drawdown statistics when they appear.
  2. Enter your crash probability views in the bucket table (or leave defaults), set the confidence slider, and watch the quintile path chart update automatically.
  3. Click Apply to options grid. This commits the GARCH/EP model and switches the grid to use it — the GARCH/EP scenario model is selected for you.
  4. Back on the Options Grid tab, adjust Roth multiplier and Contracts to match your situation, then sort by EPR.
  5. Check Sprd% and vs Theo% to filter out illiquid or unreliably-priced contracts.
  6. Use Ann% to sanity-check total cost. A 1%/yr insurance budget on a $5M portfolio is $50K/yr.

Grid columns

Option identification

Expiry
Expiration date (YYYY-MM-DD).
Ses
AM = standard SPX (cash-settled at open on expiry day). PM = SPXW weekly (settled at close). AM has less pin-risk for multi-month holds.
DTE
Days to expiration. The filter defaults to 180–540 days (6–18 months).
Strike
Option strike price in SPX index points.
OTM%
(strike / SPX − 1) × 100. A value of −20% means the strike is 20% below current SPX — roughly where a "deep correction" scenario pays off.

Market data

Mid
Mid-market price = (bid + ask) / 2, in SPX points. Multiply by 100 for dollar cost per contract.
Sprd%
Bid-ask spread as % of mid. Shown amber above 20% — the mid is unreliable at that width; expect to pay more than mid to fill.
IV
Implied volatility from the CBOE model. Higher IV = more expensive. Compare across strikes and expiries to find relatively cheap contracts.
Delta
$ change in option price per $1 move in SPX. Negative for puts. −0.15 ≈ 15% probability of finishing in-the-money (rough approximation).
Theta/d
Daily time decay in dollars per contract. LEAPS have slow theta — a key advantage for set-and-forget hedges.
OI
Open interest (total open contracts). Higher = better liquidity. Filter minimum is 100.
Vol
Today's trading volume. Thin volume is normal for LEAPS but confirms whether the mid reflects real activity.

Sizing

Cost 1c
Total premium for one contract = mid × 100.
Cost Nc
Total cost for your full position of N contracts.

Scenario payoffs

Gross payoff per contract if SPX falls by the stated amount from today's level. Deterministic — no probability weighting.

SPX −25%
"Mild crash" — your $5M portfolio would drop ~22% beta-adjusted.
SPX −40%
"Deep crash" — roughly 2008-level drawdown.
SPX −55%
"Severe crash" — Great Depression / dot-com scale. Deeply OTM puts may pay nothing at −25% but a large multiple of cost at −55%.

Expected value metrics

E[Pay]
Probability-weighted expected payoff across all scenarios, Roth-adjusted: Σ(Pi × payoffi) × Roth multiplier. This is what the put is "worth" to you given your probability model.
E[Net]
E[Pay] minus Cost 1c. Positive = positive expected value at your assumed probabilities. Most market puts have negative E[Net] at market-implied crash probabilities; a positive value means you believe crashes are underpriced.
EPR (primary sort key)
Expected Payoff Ratio = E[Pay] / Cost. The main ranking signal.
  • > 2.0 (green): excellent value — more than $2 of expected payoff per $1 spent
  • 1.0 – 2.0 (yellow): break-even to modestly positive
  • < 1.0: expensive relative to your model, but may still be worth buying for pure tail protection
EPR is most useful for comparing puts against each other — the absolute level depends on your probability inputs.
CrEff
Crash Efficiency — same as EPR but only counting crash scenarios in the numerator (bull/flat/bear excluded). Answers: "how efficiently does this put buy crash protection specifically?" A deeply OTM put has high CrEff (only pays in a real crash) but lower EPR. A put closer to the money has higher EPR but lower CrEff. Use CrEff when you care primarily about tail protection rather than overall expected value.

Cost metrics

Ann%
Annualized cost of the full position as % of the $5M portfolio = (Cost Nc / $5M) × (365 / DTE) × 100. Good tail insurance typically runs 0.5–1.5%/yr. Above 2% is expensive for a set-and-forget hedge.
vs Theo%
How much you're paying vs CBOE's own theoretical model price: (mid − theo) / theo × 100.
  • >+10%: overpaying relative to CBOE's model — wide spread or stale quote
  • Near 0: normal for liquid strikes
  • <−10%: cheaper than CBOE's model, rare but worth noting

Filters

DTE min / max
Filter by days to expiration. Default 180–540 (6–18 months). Widen to see shorter-dated puts, which are cheaper but require more frequent rolling.
OI min
Minimum open interest. Default 100. Raise to 500+ if you want only the most liquid strikes.
Vol min
Minimum today's volume. Useful for confirming that a mid-price reflects real trading, but LEAPS often trade lightly — set to 0 unless you need same-day fills.
OTM% min / max
Filter by how far out of the money the strike is. Default max is 0% (puts only — all below spot). Set min to e.g. −30% to exclude very deep strikes; set max to e.g. −10% to exclude near-the-money puts.
Session
AM = standard SPX (cash-settled at open). PM = SPXW weekly (settled at close). AM is generally preferred for multi-month holds to avoid close-of-day gamma risk near expiry.

Scenario model and probability inputs

The Scenario model selector controls how crash probabilities are assigned to each option. The GARCH/EP model (default) is the most rigorous option and is configured on the Model Builder tab — when it is active, the P(crash) and Horizon inputs have no effect and are greyed out.

If you prefer a simpler approach without running the GARCH model, you can switch to one of the two manual models:

survival
You specify P(crash) and Horizon. The model scales the crash probability to each option's DTE using a constant-hazard (Poisson) formula: a 6-month put is assigned a lower P(crash) than an 18-month put, proportional to the time window. This is the most defensible simple approach for cross-expiry comparison.
flat
The same P(crash) is applied to every option regardless of DTE. Useful as a reference or when you want to directly state a probability for a specific expiry window.
P(crash)
(survival and flat models only) Your subjective probability of a ≥20% SPX drawdown over the hold Horizon. Default 0.55. The single biggest lever — raise it if you're bearish.
Horizon (months)
(survival model only) The reference window over which your P(crash) is stated. A 6-month put in a 18-month horizon gets a lower crash probability than an 18-month put. Does not affect which options appear — use the DTE filter for that.

Other parameters

Roth multiplier
Tax advantage of a Roth IRA. A $1 payoff inside a Roth is worth more than $1 in a taxable account because gains are tax-free. At a 20% capital-gains rate, the multiplier is 1.25. Raise if you're in a higher bracket.
Contracts (0 = auto)
Number of contracts to buy. 0 auto-sizes from portfolio beta: N = ($5M × β) / (SPX × 100). At SPX ≈ 6,500 this gives ~7 contracts for a full hedge.

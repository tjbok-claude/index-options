SPX Hedge Ranker — Reference Guide

What this tool does

Fetches the live CBOE SPX options chain (delayed ~15 min), scores every put by Expected Payoff Ratio (EPR), and ranks them so you can find the most efficient tail-hedge contracts for a $5M equity portfolio held in a Roth IRA.

Parameters

P(crash)
Your subjective probability of a ≥20% SPX drawdown over the hold horizon. This is the single biggest lever — raise it if you're bearish, lower it if you're not. Default 0.55.
Horizon (months)
Your intended hold period. With the survival model, a 6-month put is assigned a lower crash probability than an 18-month put — consistent with the idea that a crash is more likely over 18 months than 6. Horizon does not change which options appear; adjust the DTE filter for that.
Roth multiplier
Tax advantage of a Roth IRA. A $1 payoff inside a Roth is worth more than $1 in a taxable account because gains are tax-free. At a 20% capital-gains rate, the multiplier is 1.25. Raise this if you're in a higher bracket.
Contracts (0 = auto)
Number of contracts to buy. 0 lets the tool auto-size based on portfolio beta: N = ($5M × β) / (SPX × 100). At SPX ≈ 6,500 this gives ~7 contracts for a full hedge.
Scenario model
survival (default): scales P(crash) to each option's DTE using a constant-hazard formula. Produces the most meaningful cross-expiry EPR comparison.
flat: applies the same P(crash) to every option regardless of DTE. Useful as a sanity check.
GARCH/EP: uses the GJR-GARCH + Entropy Pooling model you configure on the Model Builder tab. P(crash) and Horizon inputs are ignored — the model derives its own per-DTE probabilities from 100,000 simulated paths.

Grid columns

Option identification

Expiry
Expiration date (YYYY-MM-DD).
Ses
AM = standard SPX (cash-settled at open on expiry day). PM = SPXW weekly (settled at close). AM has less pin-risk for multi-month holds.
DTE
Days to expiration. The filter defaults to 180–540 days (6–18 months).
Strike
Option strike price in SPX index points.
OTM%
(strike / SPX − 1) × 100. A value of −20% means the strike is 20% below current SPX — roughly where a "deep correction" scenario pays off.

Market data

Mid
Mid-market price = (bid + ask) / 2, in SPX points. Multiply by 100 for dollar cost per contract.
Sprd%
Bid-ask spread as % of mid. Above 20% (shown amber) means the mid is unreliable — expect to pay more than mid to fill.
IV
Implied volatility from the CBOE model. Higher IV = more expensive. Compare across strikes and expiries to find relatively cheap contracts.
Delta
$ change in option price per $1 move in SPX. Negative for puts. −0.15 ≈ 15% probability of finishing in-the-money (rough approximation).
Theta/d
Daily time decay in dollars per contract. LEAPS have slow theta — a key advantage for set-and-forget hedges.
OI
Open interest (total open contracts). Higher = better liquidity. Filter minimum is 100.
Vol
Today's trading volume. Thin volume is normal for LEAPS but confirms whether the mid reflects real activity.

Sizing

Cost 1c
Total premium for one contract = mid × 100.
Cost Nc
Total cost for your full position of N contracts.

Scenario payoffs

These three columns show the gross payoff per contract (before subtracting cost) if SPX falls by the stated amount from today's level. They are deterministic — no probability weighting.

SPX −25%
Payoff if SPX drops 25%. Your $5M portfolio would drop ~22% (beta-adjusted). "Mild crash" scenario.
SPX −40%
Payoff if SPX drops 40%. "Deep crash" — roughly 2008-level drawdown.
SPX −55%
Payoff if SPX drops 55%. "Severe crash" — Great Depression / dot-com combined scale. Most OTM puts pay nothing here unless the strike is well below current levels.

Expected value metrics

E[Pay]
Probability-weighted expected payoff across all scenarios, Roth-adjusted. Formula: Σ(Pi × payoffi) × Roth multiplier. This is what the put is "worth" to you given your crash beliefs.
E[Net]
E[Pay] minus Cost 1c. Positive means the put has positive expected value at your assumed crash probability. Most market puts have negative E[Net] at market-implied crash probabilities — a positive value means you believe crashes are underpriced by the market.
EPR — Expected Payoff Ratio (primary sort key)
E[Pay] / Cost. The main ranking signal.
  • > 2.0 (green): excellent value at your crash assumptions — you're getting more than $2 of expected payoff per $1 spent
  • 1.0 – 2.0 (yellow): break-even to modestly positive
  • < 1.0: expensive relative to your beliefs, but may still be worth buying for pure tail protection
A higher P(crash) shifts EPR up for all puts. EPR is most useful for comparing puts against each other — the absolute level depends on your inputs.
CrEff — Crash Efficiency
Same formula as EPR, but only the crash scenarios contribute to the numerator (bull/flat/bear scenarios are excluded). This answers: "How efficiently does this put buy crash protection specifically?" A put deep OTM will have high CrEff (it only pays in a real crash) but low EPR (the crash scenarios don't dominate the total). A put closer to ATM has higher EPR but lower CrEff. Use CrEff when you care about pure tail protection rather than overall expected value.

Cost metrics

Ann%
Annualized cost of the full position (N contracts) as % of the $5M portfolio = (Cost Nc / $5M) × (365 / DTE) × 100. Good tail insurance typically runs 0.5–1.5%/yr. Above 2% is expensive for a set-and-forget hedge.
vs Theo%
How much you're paying vs CBOE's own theoretical (Black-Scholes-style) model price: (mid − theo) / theo × 100.
  • Red (>+10%): you're overpaying relative to CBOE's model — wide spread or stale quote
  • Near 0: normal for liquid strikes
  • Green (<−10%): cheaper than CBOE's model, rare but worth noting

How to use the rankings

  1. Set your P(crash) and Roth multiplier to match your beliefs and tax situation.
  2. Sort by EPR (default). The top rows are the most cost-efficient puts given your assumptions.
  3. Check Sprd% — avoid anything above 20% unless liquidity is acceptable.
  4. Check vs Theo% — large positive values suggest the mid is unreliable.
  5. Use Ann% to sanity-check total cost. A 1%/yr insurance budget on a $5M portfolio is $50K/yr.
  6. Compare CrEff if you want pure crash protection and are less concerned with the non-crash scenarios.
  7. For a more rigorous probability model, configure the Model Builder tab with GJR-GARCH + Entropy Pooling, then select GARCH/EP as the scenario model.
